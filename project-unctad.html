Global Cooperation in Science, Technology & Innovation for Development

Why the world's most powerful tool for solving global problems is still shared remarkably unequally.

April 2024 · Soumita Roy · 8 min read
UNCTAD, Division on Technology and Logistics
With W. K. Si Tou, A. Vezzani, and T. Pierangeli

Context Framework The Gaps Cooperation CSTD Role Recommendations Tools

In brief: This publication, prepared for the 27th session of the UN Commission on Science and Technology for Development (CSTD), examines how international cooperation in science, technology and innovation (STI) can be strengthened to help developing countries participate in the ongoing technological revolution. The benefits of frontier technologies, from AI and green energy to digital platforms, are not shared equitably: developed countries capture most of the value while developing nations risk being left further behind. The paper maps the current landscape of global STI cooperation across strategic planning, enablers, collaboration mechanisms and knowledge transfer, identifies the structural gaps that prevent developing countries from benefiting, and proposes six concrete recommendations for the international community.

The cooperation imperative

The growing complexity and speed of technological change have made international cooperation in STI not merely desirable but essential. No country, however advanced, can address climate change, pandemics or digital transformation alone. Yet the architecture of global STI cooperation is badly mismatched to the scale of the challenge. Developed countries are seizing most of the opportunities created by Industry 4.0, artificial intelligence and green technologies. Developing countries, which face the sharpest versions of the problems these technologies could solve, are precisely the ones least equipped to deploy them.

This is not a new story. Each wave of technological revolution, from the steam engine to semiconductors, has widened the gap between early adopters and the rest. What makes the current wave different is its speed and pervasiveness. AI, digital platforms and renewable energy technologies are reshaping entire economies in years rather than decades. Countries that lack the infrastructure, skills and institutional capacity to absorb these technologies risk not just falling behind but being locked into positions of permanent disadvantage.

The paper was prepared as one of two priority themes for the CSTD's 27th session, which ran from 15 to 19 April 2024 in Geneva. It draws on contributions from member states, international organisations (including the OECD, CERN, ITU and UNESCO), civil society and academia, as well as discussions at the intersessional panel held in November 2023. I contributed to the research and drafting under the guidance of Angel González-Sanz and the direction of Shamika Sirimanne.

A framework for thinking about STI cooperation

The paper organises the landscape of global STI cooperation into four pillars. This is not merely a taxonomic exercise. The framework is designed to help policymakers identify where their country's binding constraints actually sit and where international cooperation is most likely to make a difference.

Four pillars of global STI cooperation
The analytical framework used to map the current landscape and identify gaps
The framework maps STI cooperation along four dimensions. Each pillar contains specific mechanisms that the paper examines in depth, drawing on contributions from over 20 international organisations.

The first pillar, strategic planning, concerns the overall direction of international STI efforts: whether the global agenda reflects developing country priorities, whether technology foresight exercises include voices from the Global South, and whether international rules on trade, intellectual property and data governance support or hinder technology diffusion.

The second pillar covers STI enablers: digital infrastructure (including broadband, cloud computing and data interoperability), capacity building (training programmes, PhD exchanges, institutional strengthening) and investment (public R&D spending, public-private partnerships, venture capital). These are the prerequisites without which cooperation mechanisms cannot function.

The third pillar addresses collaboration mechanisms: international research networks, open science initiatives, innovation hubs and multi-stakeholder platforms. The paper draws on examples ranging from CERN's model of large-scale scientific collaboration to the CGIAR network in agricultural research and the African Union's science and technology strategies.

The fourth pillar, knowledge and technology transfer, examines how inventions and know-how move (or fail to move) across borders: licensing arrangements, IP frameworks, South-South cooperation initiatives and the role of diaspora networks in channelling expertise back to developing countries.

Where the system falls short

The paper's most striking finding is not that gaps exist (everyone knows they do) but how structural and self-reinforcing they are. Global research networks are dominated by a small number of highly interconnected institutions, overwhelmingly in North America, Western Europe and East Asia. Developing country institutions sit at the periphery of these networks, contributing less and receiving less in return. The result is that international scientific collaboration, despite rhetoric about inclusivity, tends to reproduce existing hierarchies rather than flatten them.

Official development assistance (ODA) directed at STI remains a tiny fraction of total aid flows. When it does arrive, it tends to fund discrete projects rather than building the long-term institutional capacity that would allow developing countries to participate as genuine partners in international research. The concept of international STI collaboration extends well beyond funding, but collaboration is simply not possible between partners separated by vast capability gaps. Without sustained investment in the foundational infrastructure, skills and institutions, no amount of networking will close the divide.

The paper also documents a growing tension around intellectual property. IP frameworks designed to incentivise innovation in advanced economies can become barriers to technology diffusion in developing ones. The COVID-19 pandemic brought this into sharp relief: when vaccine technologies were needed urgently across the Global South, the IP system proved slow to adapt. Similar dynamics are playing out in green technology, where the patents most relevant to climate adaptation are overwhelmingly held by firms in a handful of countries.

What works (and what does not)

The paper catalogues a wide range of existing cooperation mechanisms, from CERN's model of pooling national contributions for large-scale fundamental research to the CGIAR's distributed network of agricultural research centres in developing countries. What emerges from the survey is that the most effective initiatives share certain features: sustained funding commitments (not one-off projects), genuine participation of developing country researchers in agenda-setting (not just as data collectors), strong institutional anchoring in both sending and receiving countries, and mechanisms for translating research outputs into practical applications.

Open science initiatives, including open access publishing, shared datasets and collaborative software platforms, are narrowing some of the knowledge access gaps. But openness alone is not enough. If a country's researchers cannot access the literature but lack the laboratories, computing infrastructure or trained personnel to act on what they read, open access becomes a symbolic gesture rather than a transformative one. The paper stresses repeatedly that collaboration between partners of unequal capability requires deliberate, sustained investment in the weaker partner's capacity.

South-South cooperation receives particular attention. Countries like India, Brazil and China have developed technology transfer programmes that are sometimes better adapted to developing country conditions than North-South models. India's ITEC programme, for instance, has trained thousands of professionals from across Africa and Asia in fields ranging from IT to renewable energy. The paper argues that these South-South channels deserve more institutional support and better integration into the global STI architecture.

What role for the CSTD?

The final analytical section of the paper examines the CSTD's own contribution to facilitating global STI cooperation. The Commission, a subsidiary body of ECOSOC with its secretariat housed at UNCTAD, has launched several pilot programmes for international collaboration: the CropWatch system for agricultural monitoring using satellite data, a PhD programme for young scientists from developing countries in collaboration with Okayama University, and geospatial technology projects aimed at building resilience in small island developing states.

The paper argues that the CSTD is uniquely positioned to play a coordinating role: it is the only UN body mandated specifically to address STI for development, it convenes an unusually broad range of stakeholders (governments, international organisations, academia, civil society and the private sector), and its intersessional panels provide a mechanism for sustained dialogue between annual sessions. What it lacks is the financial resources and institutional weight to translate its convening power into programmatic scale. The paper suggests that strengthening the CSTD's capacity to deliver technology foresight and assessment, and to coordinate international responses to emerging technologies, would be a high-value, relatively low-cost investment for the international community.

Six recommendations

Recommendation 1

Adopt an inclusive international STI agenda that reflects developing country priorities, with mechanisms for their genuine participation in setting research directions and governance frameworks.

Recommendation 2

Establish a multilateral technology foresight and assessment system, coordinated through the CSTD, to help developing countries anticipate technological disruptions and position themselves to benefit from emerging opportunities.

Recommendation 3

Build enabling digital and skill environments. Investment in broadband infrastructure, digital literacy and STEM education is the prerequisite for any meaningful STI cooperation.

Recommendation 4

Increase the share of official development assistance dedicated to STI, and channel it towards institutional capacity building rather than discrete projects.

Recommendation 5

Strengthen research networks and collaboration among different actors, with deliberate measures to include developing country institutions as equal partners, not peripheral participants.

Recommendation 6

Promote technology and knowledge transfer through supportive IP frameworks, South-South cooperation channels, and diaspora engagement programmes that channel expertise back to developing countries.
