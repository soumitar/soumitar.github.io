Chinese Development Finance: Investment and Forests in Asia

Does Beijing's $128 billion in financing attract foreign investment, accelerate deforestation, or both?

June 2024 · Soumita Roy · 12 min read
Geneva Graduate Institute (IHEID) · Master in International Economics

In Brief Background Data Empirical Strategy Results Implications

In Brief: Between 2000 and 2015, China committed roughly $128 billion in official financing to 31 developing countries across Asia. This project asks two questions: did that money attract foreign direct investment, and did it contribute to forest cover loss? Using project-level data from AidData, FDI figures from UNCTAD, and satellite-derived deforestation data from Global Forest Watch, I find a significant positive relationship between Chinese financing and FDI inflows, particularly through commercially oriented (OOF-like) flows and the production and services sector. I find no statistically significant effect on deforestation. The results carry a caveat: the instrumental variables used to address endogeneity are weak, so the findings should be read as suggestive rather than causal.

The quiet reshaping of Asian development

China's emergence as a major development financier has upended a system that Western donors and multilateral institutions controlled for decades. Under the Belt and Road Initiative, adopted in 2013, Beijing committed over $1 trillion globally to infrastructure, connectivity and trade facilitation. The ambition is continental in scale: roads, railways, ports and power plants spanning more than 60 countries, with Asia as the strategic centre of gravity.

Yet the academic literature has focused disproportionately on Africa. Studies of Chinese financing in that continent are plentiful, examining everything from growth effects to elite capture to labour market disruptions. Asia, which received roughly a quarter of all Chinese development commitments between 2009 and 2015, remains comparatively understudied. This is a gap worth closing. The region's economies are larger, its environmental vulnerabilities more acute, and the geopolitical dynamics around Chinese financing more complex than in any other part of the developing world.

The financing itself is distinctive. Unlike Western aid, which typically comes with governance conditionalities, Chinese official financing operates under more flexible terms. It is categorised into three types following OECD-DAC conventions: ODA-like flows (concessional, development-focused), OOF-like flows (commercially oriented, less concessional), and "Vague" flows that lack sufficient transparency for definitive classification. Between 2000 and 2015, OOF-like flows and loans dominated, particularly after 2007, reflecting Beijing's preference for commercially structured financing tied to strategic infrastructure.

Within the 31 Asian countries in this study, the sectoral distribution is revealing. Nearly half of all commitments, about $80 billion, went to physical infrastructure: transport, energy, communications and water supply. Production and services (agriculture, mining, construction) accounted for 18 percent, and human capital (education and health) for 25 percent. The remainder was spread across governance, emergency response and debt management. This concentration in infrastructure is central to the empirical story: it is precisely the kind of spending most likely to attract foreign investors, and most likely to cause environmental disruption.

Pakistan, Myanmar and Cambodia stand out as major recipients, all strategically positioned along the Belt and Road's logistics corridors. India is excluded from the sample: its economy is large enough and its geopolitical relationship with China tense enough that Chinese financing plays a proportionally minor role. High-income Asian economies are also excluded. What remains is a set of 31 developing countries where Chinese financing is large relative to the economy and directly observable in its effects.

Data: three datasets, one question

The analysis links three datasets at the country-year level. The primary explanatory variable is Chinese official financing per capita, lagged by two years to allow for the gestation period between financial commitment and economic effect. I use both the dollar value and the project count as alternative measures, since roughly 15 percent of project records in the dataset lack financial values.

Chinese Development Finance. The AidData Global Chinese Development Finance Dataset (Version 2) provides project-level records of Chinese financing commitments from 2000 to 2017. Compiled using the Tracking Underreported Financial Flows (TUFF) methodology, the dataset draws on Chinese ministry records, recipient country finance ministries, news reports in English, Chinese and local languages, and NGO case studies. I imported the geoJSON project location files into ArcGIS, performed spatial joins to link projects to administrative boundaries, and restructured the data into a country-year panel. The analysis uses commitments through 2015, with the two-year lag pushing the outcome window to 2017.

Foreign Direct Investment. FDI inward flow data come from UNCTAD's World Investment Report, covering 2002 to 2017. I construct three outcome measures: FDI per capita in USD millions, FDI as a percentage of GDP, and the log of FDI inflows. These capture different dimensions of investment climate response: the absolute level, the proportional weight in the economy, and the growth rate.

Forest Cover Loss. Deforestation data come from the Global Forest Watch platform, which uses Landsat satellite imagery to measure annual tree cover loss at 30-metre resolution. I measure forest loss in hectares and as a percentage of the country's total forest area in 2000, the baseline year. This dual measure captures both absolute environmental impact and proportional degradation.

The identification problem

The central empirical challenge is endogeneity. China does not allocate financing randomly. Countries that receive more Chinese money may already be on trajectories of rising FDI or accelerating deforestation, for reasons unrelated to the financing itself. A naive regression of FDI on Chinese financing would confound the effect of the money with whatever made the country attractive to both Chinese and other financiers in the first place.

I address this with three approaches, each more demanding than the last.

Approach 1: panel regression with fixed effects

The baseline specification is an OLS regression with country and year fixed effects. The outcome variable (FDI or forest loss) in country i at time t is regressed on the two-year lagged per capita Chinese official financing, controlling for lagged log population and lagged log GDP. Country fixed effects absorb all time-invariant differences between countries (geography, institutional quality, colonial history). Year fixed effects absorb global shocks (commodity price cycles, financial crises) that affect all countries simultaneously. What remains is within-country, within-year variation: the question becomes whether a country that receives more Chinese financing than usual, relative to its own average, subsequently sees higher FDI or more deforestation than usual.

The baseline equation: Yi,t = β1 OFi,t-2 + β2 log(popi,t-1) + β3 log(GDPi,t-1) + ηi + ηt + εi,t

Where OF is per capita Chinese official financing committed two years prior, and ηi and ηt are country and year fixed effects.

Approach 2: instrumental variables

Fixed effects control for unobserved heterogeneity, but not for time-varying confounders that correlate with both Chinese financing and FDI. To push closer to causal identification, I use an instrumental variable strategy. The instrument interacts two components: the supply of Chinese financing (measured by the lagged detrended change in China's net foreign exchange reserves, which proxy for Beijing's capacity to fund overseas projects) and the demand for it (measured by a country's voting alignment with China in the United Nations General Assembly, which proxies for the political likelihood of receiving Chinese aid).

The logic is that fluctuations in China's reserves reflect its fiscal capacity to lend abroad, while UNGA alignment captures the political channel through which financing flows. The interaction of the two should predict how much financing a country receives without directly affecting its FDI or deforestation outcomes except through the financing itself. In the first stage, the instrument predicts per capita Chinese financing. In the second stage, the fitted values from the first stage are used to estimate the causal effect on FDI and forest cover.

I also test an alternative proxy for demand: the historical probability of a country having received Chinese aid in prior years. Both instruments yield qualitatively similar results, though both suffer from low first-stage F-statistics, indicating weak instruments. This is the study's primary methodological limitation and the reason I characterise the findings as suggestive rather than definitively causal.

Approach 3: sectoral decomposition

To investigate which types of financing drive the results, I decompose the aggregate per capita CDF measure into three sectoral components: infrastructure (transport, energy, communications, water), social infrastructure (education, health, social services), and production and services (agriculture, mining, construction, business services). A multivariate OLS regression with all three sectors as separate regressors, plus the usual fixed effects and controls, reveals which channels are doing the heavy lifting.

Finding 1: Chinese financing boosts FDI

The OLS results are clear. For each additional $1 million per capita of Chinese official financing committed two years prior, FDI per capita rises by $0.057 million, significant at the 1 percent level. FDI as a share of GDP increases by 0.001 percentage points (significant at 5 percent), and log FDI inflows rise at the 5 percent level. The IV estimates are larger in magnitude, as is typical when OLS is biased downwards by endogeneity, but the weak instruments mean these should be interpreted cautiously.

Finding 2: the type of financing matters

Breaking the aggregate down by financing type reveals a sharp asymmetry. OOF-like flows, the commercially oriented kind, drive the entire effect. Each additional $1 million per capita of OOF committed two years prior raises FDI per capita by $0.058 million (significant at 1 percent) and FDI as a share of GDP by 0.001 percentage points. ODA-like flows, the concessional development aid, show no significant impact on any FDI metric. This makes economic sense: OOF-like financing targets infrastructure and business sectors that directly improve the investment climate, while ODA-like flows tend toward social programmes with less immediate bearing on investor decisions.

Production and services

The strongest sectoral effect. Each additional $1 million per capita in this sector raises FDI per capita by $0.053 million (p < 0.01). This sector includes agriculture, mining, construction and business services.

Infrastructure

Positive effect on FDI as a share of GDP: 0.0044 percentage points per additional $1 million per capita (p < 0.01). Transport, energy and communications investments improve the operating environment for foreign firms.

Social infrastructure

No significant effect on any FDI metric. Education and health spending may improve long-term human capital but does not produce the short-run investment climate effects visible in a two-year lag window.

OOF vs. ODA

Commercially oriented OOF-like flows drive the entire aggregate effect. Concessional ODA-like flows show no significant relationship with FDI, consistent with their focus on social rather than economic infrastructure.

Finding 3: no detectable effect on deforestation

The environmental question yields a null result. Neither the OLS nor the IV specifications find a statistically significant relationship between Chinese official financing and forest cover loss, whether measured in hectares or as a percentage of the 2000 baseline. The coefficients are small and the standard errors large. This does not mean Chinese financing has no environmental impact; it means that across 31 countries and 16 years of data, the aggregate relationship is too noisy to detect.

Several explanations are plausible. The environmental effects of large infrastructure projects may be highly localised, visible at the project site but washed out in country-level data. Alternatively, the positive and negative environmental channels may roughly cancel each other: road-building opens forest frontiers, but improved agricultural infrastructure reduces the need to expand into new land. The result is consistent with the study's second hypothesis, which predicted that the net effect would depend on the nature of the financed projects and the strength of local environmental governance.

What this means for policy

The results point in two directions. On the investment side, Chinese development finance appears to do what its proponents claim: improve the environment for foreign investment, particularly when the money goes to commercially productive sectors. The mechanism is intuitive. Better roads, more reliable electricity and improved telecommunications reduce the cost of doing business, attract multinational firms, and signal that a country is open for investment. The fact that OOF-like flows drive the effect while ODA-like flows do not reinforces this interpretation: it is the infrastructure and business-oriented spending, not the social development spending, that moves the needle on FDI.

On the environmental side, the absence of a detectable aggregate effect should not be read as reassurance. It may reflect the limitations of country-level data rather than the absence of real environmental harm. Future research using sub-national or project-level geospatial data, linking specific Chinese-financed infrastructure projects to satellite-measured deforestation in their immediate vicinity, would provide a more decisive test.

Limitations to note: The instrumental variables suffer from low first-stage F-statistics (3.36 to 3.64), indicating weak instruments. This means the IV estimates may be biased and should be treated as suggestive rather than definitive. The study uses commitment amounts rather than actual disbursements, since transaction data are often unavailable. And the two-year lag, while standard in the literature, may not fully capture the gestation period of large infrastructure investments.

For policymakers in recipient countries, the findings suggest that Chinese financing can be a genuine catalyst for economic integration, but that its benefits are concentrated in specific sectors. Strategic allocation of financing toward productive infrastructure, combined with environmental safeguards for large-scale projects, offers the best prospect for capturing the investment gains without incurring ecological costs. For researchers, the weak-instrument problem points to the need for better identification strategies, perhaps exploiting quasi-natural experiments arising from policy shifts in Beijing or from the staggered rollout of Belt and Road projects across countries.
