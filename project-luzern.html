Innovation Landscape of the Luzern Region

Mapping a regional innovation ecosystem through multi-source data analysis.

KOF Swiss Economic Institute, ETH Zürich · January 2026
Co-authors: T. Hütner Reisch, M. König, M. Salvetti, M. Wörter

Overview

This project started with a practical question from policymakers: where should a region invest to strengthen its innovation capacity? Rather than relying on anecdotal impressions or looking at one sector in isolation, we set out to build a comprehensive, data-driven picture of the regional economy, covering its industrial structure, technological profile, scientific output, and workforce skills, all at the firm level.

What makes this study distinctive is the breadth of data we brought together. No single dataset tells you enough: patent data misses service innovation, publication data misses firms that don't collaborate with universities, and industry classifications don't capture what companies actually make. So we combined eight complementary sources to triangulate where the real strengths and opportunities lie, and where the gaps are.

Approach

The study follows a three-phase structure: (1) a comprehensive stocktaking of the existing economic and innovation structure, (2) identification of thematic clusters with future potential, and (3) policy-oriented fields of action. All analyses were conducted at the firm level wherever possible, combining multiple complementary datasets to build a picture that no single source could provide alone.

The guiding analytical insight is that regional diversification rarely happens into completely unrelated activities. Instead, new strengths tend to emerge where there is cognitive, technological, or organisational proximity to existing capabilities — an idea rooted in the economic complexity and relatedness literature. We used this framework to distinguish realistic growth opportunities from isolated observations.

Data Sources

A key design choice was the integration of eight distinct data layers, each capturing a different facet of the innovation ecosystem. No single dataset is decisive — the clusters emerge from patterns that recur across multiple sources.

Patent Data
PATSTAT + Bureau van Dijk Orbis IP. Firm-patent linkages, IPC classification, time-series analysis (2010–2022).
Scientific Publications
OpenAlex bibliometric database. Topic, field, and domain classification. Growth analysis (2010–2024).
Product Portfolios
AI-extracted from firm websites using KOF's web scraping pipeline. Employment-weighted product mapping.
Industrial Structure
NOGA classification via Orbis. Sectoral employment, value creation, and RCA calculation.
Innosuisse Projects
Science–industry cooperation data. Project applications (2016–2025) mapped to thematic clusters.
Human Resources
Revelio Labs person-level data. Workforce composition, role profiles, and skill matching to clusters.
KOF Innovation Indicators
Firm size, productivity (value added/employee), and the KOF innovation web indicator for innovation propensity.
Future Technologies (ASPI)
Keyword-based classification inspired by ASPI's Critical Technology Tracker. Multilingual keywords applied to firm websites.
Data integration pipeline — eight complementary sources feed into RCA, RTA, and relatedness analysis to identify innovation theme clusters, validated by expert interviews.

Methodology

The analytical backbone combines established methods from innovation economics with computational approaches:

Revealed Comparative Advantage (RCA) measures whether a sector, product, or skill is overrepresented in the region relative to the national average. This identifies existing strengths across industries and products.

Revealed Technological Advantage (RTA) applies the same logic to patent data, comparing the region's technological profile against the national patent portfolio to detect areas of above-average technological specialisation.

Figure 10 — Patent structure by IPC section and subclass. The treemap shows the relative composition of patent filings (2010–2022), with colours corresponding to IPC sections (e.g. G – Physics, H – Electricity, B – Performing Operations/Transport). This view reveals the technological profile underlying the region's innovation activity.

Relatedness analysis maps how close different industries, products, and technologies are to each other based on their statistical co-occurrence across regions and firms. This makes it possible to identify realistic paths for diversification — fields that are technologically adjacent to existing strengths and therefore plausible targets for growth.

Figure 4 — The industry space. Each node represents a 4-digit NOGA industry class. Classes are positioned closer together when they require similar qualification profiles. Nodes with black outlines indicate overrepresentation (RCA > 1). Colours correspond to NOGA sections. This visualisation reveals ecosystems of related industries rather than isolated sectors.
Figure 12 — The technology (patent) space. Each node represents an IPC subclass (4-digit). Related patent classes appear closer together based on co-classification patterns. Nodes with black outlines indicate overrepresentation (RTA > 1). Colours correspond to IPC chapters. This map complements the industry space by revealing the technological dimension of regional capabilities.

Product space analysis, inspired by the Atlas of Economic Complexity, maps the region's product portfolio and identifies adjacent product classes that represent plausible diversification opportunities.

ASPI-inspired keyword classification connects the local firm base with strategically relevant future technologies. Because ASPI does not publish its keyword dictionaries, we constructed our own — generating detailed keyword lists covering technologies, tools, processes, materials, standards, and technical variants, then translating them into German, French, and Italian for application to firm website content.

Figure 5 — Growth opportunities. The x-axis shows relative presence (RCA) compared to the Swiss average; the y-axis shows technological proximity to the region's existing strengths. The four quadrants map Strengths, Opportunities, Weaknesses, and Threats — providing a strategic lens for identifying both established advantages and realistic growth paths.

Finally, the quantitative analysis was complemented by qualitative expert interviews with practitioners from the identified innovation fields, grounding the data in real-world constraints around scaling, integration challenges, regulation, and talent.

Policy Relevance

The study synthesised these data layers to identify thematic innovation clusters — areas where industrial activity, technological capability, scientific output, workforce skills, and future-oriented technologies converge. The resulting clusters are not arbitrary groupings but structured combinations of adjacent capabilities already present in the region, supported by evidence of innovation activity and growth potential.

A key insight is that innovation support should often be less focused on individual technologies and more focused on problems, interfaces, and integration. The greatest innovation potential frequently lies not in developing entirely new components but in intelligently combining existing ones. Clusters need visibility, talent, test environments, institutional bridges, practical experimentation, and demand-side signals — not just research funding.

The report proposes cluster-specific fields of action, taking into account differences in firm-size structure, integration challenges, regulatory environments, and links to higher education. Concrete instruments are intended to be developed jointly with local support bodies, firms, associations, and universities.
