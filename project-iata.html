PaxInsight: Real-Time Passenger Satisfaction Intelligence

How IATA's platform captures honest, comparable airline customer experience data at scale—and translates it into actionable strategy.

April 2024 — March 2025 · Soumita Roy · 9 min read
International Air Transport Association (IATA), Geneva

Overview Methodology Survey Demo Dashboards Impact

Project Overview: PaxInsight is IATA's proprietary passenger satisfaction benchmarking platform. Over 12 months as a Market Researcher in the Survey Solutions unit, I designed and operated the survey methodology, built end-to-end data pipelines in R, and delivered monthly analytics dashboards to three major airlines. This case study walks through the complete project: from sampling methodology and survey design to interactive dashboards and strategic business impact.

My Contribution

  • Designed the survey methodology: stratification logic, question branching, and sampling frames for 47 routes across 3 cabin classes
  • Built end-to-end data pipelines in R — from raw survey ingestion and cleaning to automated monthly reporting scripts
  • Created and maintained Power BI dashboards (87 visualisations per client), delivering monthly executive intelligence packages
  • Conducted ad-hoc analysis on client requests (turnaround within 48 hours): route comparisons, cohort deep-dives, and competitive benchmarking

How airlines lost sight of what matters

The airline industry is obsessed with measurement. Fuel efficiency, on-time performance, load factors, revenue per available seat-mile—the metrics flow endlessly from the operations centre into executive dashboards. Airlines can tell you the precise temperature in the cargo hold and the exact moment an engine began to degrade. Yet somewhere amid this precision lies a profound blind spot: they have no reliable idea whether their customers are actually satisfied.

Most airline surveys suffer from deep methodological problems. In-flight surveys reach only the motivated minority—the extremely satisfied or extremely angry. Business-class passengers respond at different rates than economy. Responses arrive weeks later, by which time operational context is lost. One carrier might think a mechanical delay caused satisfaction to plummet, when in reality the sample just happened to oversample disgruntled passengers.

PaxInsight was designed to close this gap. Created by IATA and operated across a global network of airlines, the platform addresses the fundamental asymmetry: while airlines invest billions in seats and catering, they invest almost nothing in understanding whether those investments actually improve passenger experience in any measurable, comparable way.

The core innovation: Stratified random sampling from flight manifests eliminates self-selection bias. Post-flight timing (24–48 hours) ensures recency. IATA's neutral role ensures comparability across competitors. The result: the first genuinely representative measure of airline customer experience.

The methodology: why sampling bias matters

Consider a long-haul flight that encountered a 2-hour mechanical delay. An in-flight survey shows low satisfaction. But which passengers respond? The irate ones, disproportionately. The neutral ones, who saw the delay as philosophically inevitable? Unlikely to complete a survey mid-flight. The result: the airline thinks the delay caused catastrophic experience damage, when in reality the experience decline was moderate. The survey measured complaint propensity, not experience.

PaxInsight eliminates this through methodological rigour. Passengers are selected via stratified random sampling from actual flight manifests. No self-selection. The survey arrives 24–48 hours post-flight, when the experience is salient but the passenger is calm. Response rates average 8–12% (above industry norms), yielding representative samples of 150+ responses per airline–region–class–month combination. And because all airlines are measured identically, benchmarking becomes meaningful.

Survey architecture: capturing the complete journey

Rather than ask a single satisfaction question, PaxInsight captures feedback across 60+ attributes spanning eight journey stages. But asking all questions to all passengers would create fatigue and dropout. Instead, branching logic personalises: economy passengers see different questions than business. Short-haul omits lounge. Long-haul adds sleep quality. The result is a survey tailored to each passenger, with completion rates exceeding 94%.

One client discovered that business-class satisfaction was driven entirely by service consistency (crew attentiveness, meal timing) rather than seat recline or champagne selection. Another found that economy passengers on short-haul flights prioritised boarding speed over in-flight entertainment. These insights would be invisible in a generic “are you happy?” survey. They required deliberately designed questions targeting specific journey moments.

Impact: Data into Strategy

Over 12 months, I delivered monthly intelligence products to three major airlines. Each package included an 8–12 page executive report, real-time Power BI dashboards with drill-down capability, and ad-hoc analysis with a 48-hour turnaround. Below are representative examples of how the data translated into measurable business decisions.

Premium Economy Repositioning

Route-level analysis revealed that Premium Economy satisfaction was driven by seat space—not lounge access, which scored lowest. One airline shifted marketing emphasis from lounge benefits to legroom and privacy, aligning communications with what passengers actually valued.

Crew Scheduling Optimisation

Cohort analysis showed that crew attentiveness scores dropped systematically on routes with tight turnaround schedules. The data supported a case for adjusted crew rest policies on high-frequency long-haul pairings.

Competitive Route Strategy

Benchmarking revealed one client consistently outperformed competitors on Transpacific routes but underperformed on Intra-Europe. This informed a strategic decision to protect long-haul market share while investing in short-haul service improvements.

Capacity–Satisfaction Threshold

Monthly trend analysis identified that satisfaction declined when load factors exceeded 80%, particularly in lounge and meal service scores. This established a data-backed threshold for capacity management decisions.

The work demonstrated that rigorously collected passenger experience data—stratified, timed, and benchmarked—can inform strategic decisions that airlines previously made on intuition. Each of these outcomes was supported by specific data points from the monthly reporting pipeline I built and maintained.
