PaxInsight: Passenger Experience Analytics

Designing survey solutions and delivering market intelligence for global airline customer experience benchmarking.

April 2024 — March 2025 · Soumita Roy · 9 min read
International Air Transport Association (IATA), Geneva
Market Researcher, Survey Solutions Unit

In brief: At IATA's Survey Solutions unit, I worked on PaxInsight, a proprietary platform that captures real-time passenger satisfaction data across global airlines. The role combined survey design, statistical methodology, data visualisation, and client strategy. I delivered customised dashboards using Power BI, provided market intelligence to airline clients, and managed three concurrent projects worth combined annual revenue of €300k+. The work sits at the intersection of applied econometrics, consumer behaviour measurement, and competitive benchmarking.

The challenge: measuring passenger experience at scale

Airlines face a deceptively simple problem: they know how many seats they sold, but not what their customers actually thought of the experience. Traditional satisfaction metrics—Net Promoter Score, overall satisfaction ratings—are readily available. What's rare is granular, comparable, real-time data that lets airlines see how they stack up against competitors, segment by segment, route by route, class by class.

The market is fragmented. Some carriers use in-flight surveys (low response rates, sampling bias). Others license panel data (expensive, outdated, non-representative of actual flight rosters). A few conduct proprietary research, but that data never leaves the boardroom. IATA's insight: the industry needs a neutral, standardised platform that collects verified feedback from actual passengers—captured within 24–48 hours of landing—and benchmarks performance across airlines at granular geographic and operational levels.

This is PaxInsight. And it required solving three hard problems: representativeness (sampling the right passengers), timeliness (getting them to respond within the critical window), and comparability (making data from airline A directly comparable to airline B).

Survey design and sampling methodology

The foundation is rigorous sampling. Here's how it works: IATA receives monthly schedule data from each participating airline. For a given airline, route, and class combination, we define the universe of flights. Then, using stratified random sampling, we select a representative subset of passengers based on their ticket numbers. This ensures the sample reflects the airline's actual passenger mix—not their willingness to respond to an in-flight popup, which is self-selected and biased.

Why this matters: If an airline carries 70% economy, 20% premium economy, and 10% business class passengers, the sample should mirror that distribution. In-flight surveys often oversample business class (higher engagement) and undercount economy. Our method is neutral and verifiable.

The survey itself covers the entire passenger journey. We ask about 60+ attributes across eight touchpoints: booking experience, check-in, lounge (if applicable), pre-flight amenities, in-flight comfort and service, meal quality, entertainment, connectivity, baggage handling, and arrival experience. But we don't ask all passengers all questions. Instead, we adapt the questionnaire based on their class of service and route type, using branching logic to keep response time under 5 minutes.

Timelines matter. Surveys are sent within 24–48 hours of landing—the window when the experience is still salient but the passenger is no longer emotionally exhausted. Response rates hover around 8–12%, which is above industry average for cold-contact surveys, because the invitation comes from IATA (a trusted third party) and because we've optimised the survey for mobile (80% of responses come from phones).

Granular data dimensions

The real power of PaxInsight lies in its granularity. You don't just get an overall satisfaction score. You get satisfaction broken down by:

Route geography
Intra-Europe, Transatlantic North, Transatlantic South, Transpacific, etc. Because travel experience differs radically by distance.
Cabin class
Economy, Premium Economy, Business, First. Service expectations and satisfaction drivers are entirely different.
Journey stage
Pre-flight, inflight, ground services. Allows airlines to see exactly where experience breaks down.
Passenger profile
Frequency (first time vs. frequent), purpose (leisure vs. business), origin/destination market.

Clients can compare themselves against "all airlines," "aggregated competitors," or "targeted airlines" of their choosing. This flexibility is crucial: a long-haul carrier cares less about their score against regional airlines and more about how they compare to peers on the London–New York route.

Role and deliverables

I served as the analyst-strategist between the product team and airline clients. The work had four components:

1. Survey customisation and statistical design. Working with clients, I identified which metrics mattered most to their strategy. For a low-cost carrier, on-time performance and seat comfort dominate. For a premium carrier, lounge access and personalised service are critical. I selected the subset of questions to include in each client's version of the survey, ensuring we captured their strategic priorities while maintaining methodological rigour. This meant understanding both the survey's statistical design (sample size, power calculations, margin of error) and the client's business model.

2. Data analysis and reporting. Once survey responses came in, I cleaned and processed the data in R and Excel. The work was unglamorous but essential: handling missing values, flag inconsistencies (respondent claims a 90-minute flight took 4 hours; flag it), and computing derived metrics. I calculated satisfaction scores by segment, computed year-over-year trends, and identified statistically significant changes in client performance.

3. Dashboard design and visualisation. I built interactive Power BI dashboards for three major clients. These dashboards let airline executives drill down from "overall satisfaction" to "how satisfied were business-class passengers on routes where we face direct competition?" Real-time filtering, trend visualisation, and benchmark overlays. The goal was decision-ready intelligence, not academic precision tables.

4. Client management and strategy. I oversaw project delivery for three concurrent clients, managed timelines, handled their questions, and occasionally counselled them on how to interpret results. One client was misinterpreting a flat-looking benchmark line as "no improvement"; it was actually statistically stable performance in a volatile market. Understanding the difference between "nothing changed" and "we held steady against turbulence" is how data becomes strategic.

What the data revealed

Across the clients I worked with, several patterns emerged—useful both for them and for understanding airline economics more broadly.

Premium cabin satisfaction is driven by service consistency, not amenities. Airlines assume more amenities (bigger seats, premium meals) drive satisfaction. In reality, our data showed that business-class passengers value predictable, reliable service. A flight delayed by 3 hours with courteous updates scored higher on satisfaction than a perfectly on-time flight where cabin crew were inconsistent. The lesson: culture and training matter more than spending on hard products.
Economy passengers are heterogeneous. Industry benchmarks often treat economy as one block. But segmenting by trip distance revealed huge differences. On short-haul flights (under 3 hours), passengers prioritise efficiency: quick boarding, on-time departure, and getting luggage fast. On long-haul, comfort features and entertainment became important. Airlines were overfitting to one segment and confusing their broader economy base.
Digital experience is now table stakes. Every client reported that Wi-Fi availability and quality had moved from a "nice to have" to a primary driver of satisfaction. Passengers didn't just want internet; they had high expectations around speed. Carriers investing in seatback entertainment but neglecting Wi-Fi were leaving satisfaction on the table.

Technical approach

The analytical pipeline combined industry-standard survey methods with targeted econometric approaches:

Stratified Random Sampling Survey Design (Branching Logic) Response Weighting Likert Scale Analysis Benchmark Aggregation Time-Series Decomposition Multivariate Segmentation
R (tidyverse, ggplot2) Excel (pivot tables, scenario modeling) Power BI (DAX, M queries) Survey Logic (Qualtrics-adjacent platforms)

Response weighting deserves a mention. Because we stratify by route and class, but response rates vary by segment, we apply inverse probability weights. If business-class passengers on Transatlantic routes have a 6% response rate but economy passengers on the same route have 12%, we weight business responses upward to correct for this selectivity. This keeps the sample representative of the actual flight roster, even as response rates fluctuate.

Key outputs

Over the 12-month engagement, I delivered:

Monthly benchmark reports for three airline clients, each 8–12 pages, including overall satisfaction scores, drill-downs by route and class, competitive positioning, and actionable insights. These were presented to C-suite and board-level decision-makers.

Three custom Power BI dashboards with real-time data refresh, allowing executives to monitor satisfaction trends continuously rather than waiting for monthly reports.

Ad-hoc analyses for specific questions. One client wanted to understand why satisfaction dropped on Friday evening flights. I ran a segmented analysis and discovered it wasn't the flights themselves—it was that Friday flights carried higher proportions of business passengers, who had different expectations. The solution wasn't operational; it was about messaging and service design.

Product strategy input to IATA's product team. As I worked with clients, I documented feature requests and methodological refinements. This fed back into the product roadmap, ensuring PaxInsight evolved to meet market needs.

Why this work matters

Passenger surveys might sound like a peripheral market research exercise. They're not. Here's why this matters for the airline industry and beyond:

Accountability and transparency. Airlines have long claimed they prioritise customer experience, but without third-party measurement, those claims are unverifiable. PaxInsight is overseen by IATA, a neutral party. The data is independently collected, standardised, and benchmarked. This transparency improves industry-wide service quality by making performance visible.

Route and network economics. Satisfaction is correlated with loyalty, repeat purchase, and word-of-mouth. Airlines now have granular data linking satisfaction to network profitability. A carrier might discover that their San Francisco–London route has lower satisfaction than peers, despite similar on-time performance. That's a signal to revisit that route's product strategy, staffing, or even pricing power.

Passenger heterogeneity. The airline industry traditionally segments by class. But within each class, passengers are diverse—trip distance, frequency, purpose all matter. Better measurement of what drives satisfaction across these segments allows airlines to tailor offerings more precisely, improving overall utilisation of premium cabin capacity.

What I learned

This work taught me three things that span from market research to economics to broader business strategy:

Methodology is strategy. How you measure shapes what you manage. The choice of sampling method, survey timing, and benchmark aggregation isn't neutral—it determines what insights emerge. Working with clients, I realised that many were accustomed to internal surveys or panel data that biased them toward certain conclusions. Switching to a standardised methodology changed what they saw. This extends beyond surveys: any firm's choice of metrics (NPS vs. CSAT vs. retention) reflects underlying strategic assumptions.

Data quality beats sophistication. I was trained as an econometrician, which means I'm inclined toward complexity—multivariate models, causal inference, Bayesian methods. In this role, I learned that clean, representative data driving simple, interpretable metrics often outperforms flashy analysis on messy data. Airlines don't need econometric models; they need to know "on this route, we're in the 65th percentile for satisfaction, and here are the three drivers." Getting the numerator right (representative sample) matters more than getting the denominator complicated.

Business context is essential. My training is in economics and statistics. But the role also required understanding airline operations, revenue management, and network strategy. A finding like "satisfaction dropped" is meaningless without context: Was it because of an operational failure? Market composition? Changed expectations? The ability to translate data into business decisions requires understanding both the numbers and the business they describe.
