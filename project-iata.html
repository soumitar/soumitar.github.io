PaxInsight: Real-Time Passenger Satisfaction Intelligence

How IATA's platform captures honest, comparable airline customer experience data at scale—and translates it into actionable strategy.

April 2024 — March 2025 · Soumita Roy · 9 min read
International Air Transport Association (IATA), Geneva

Project Overview: PaxInsight is IATA's proprietary passenger satisfaction benchmarking platform. Over 12 months as a Market Researcher in the Survey Solutions unit, I designed and operated the survey methodology, built end-to-end data pipelines in R, and delivered monthly analytics dashboards to three major airlines. This case study walks through the complete project: from sampling methodology and survey design to interactive dashboards and strategic business impact. The page includes an interactive survey demo and real-time visualisations illustrating the scope and depth of the work.

How airlines lost sight of what matters

The airline industry is obsessed with measurement. Fuel efficiency, on-time performance, load factors, revenue per available seat-mile—the metrics flow endlessly from the operations centre into executive dashboards. Airlines can tell you the precise temperature in the cargo hold and the exact moment an engine began to degrade. Yet somewhere amid this precision lies a profound blind spot: they have no reliable idea whether their customers are actually satisfied.

Most airline surveys suffer from methodological cancer. In-flight surveys reach only the motivated minority—the extremely satisfied or extremely angry. Business-class passengers respond at different rates than economy. Responses arrive weeks later, by which time operational context is lost. One carrier might think a mechanical delay caused satisfaction to plummet, when in reality the sample just happened to oversample disgruntled passengers.

PaxInsight was designed to close this gap. Created by IATA and operated across a global network of airlines, the platform addresses the fundamental asymmetry: while airlines invest billions in seats and catering, they invest almost nothing in understanding whether those investments actually improve passenger experience in any measurable, comparable way.

The core innovation: Stratified random sampling from flight manifests eliminates self-selection bias. Post-flight timing (24–48 hours) ensures recency. IATA's neutral role ensures comparability across competitors. The result: the first genuinely representative measure of airline customer experience.

The methodology: why sampling bias matters

Consider a long-haul flight that encountered a 2-hour mechanical delay. An in-flight survey shows low satisfaction. But which passengers respond? The irate ones, disproportionately. The neutral ones, who saw the delay as philosophically inevitable? Unlikely to complete a survey mid-flight. The result: the airline thinks the delay caused catastrophic experience damage, when in reality the experience decline was moderate. The survey measured complaint propensity, not experience.

PaxInsight eliminates this through methodological rigour. Passengers are selected via stratified random sampling from actual flight manifests. No self-selection. The survey arrives 24–48 hours post-flight, when the experience is salient but the passenger is calm. Response rates average 8–12% (above industry norms), yielding representative samples of 150+ responses per airline–region–class–month combination. And because all airlines are measured identically, benchmarking becomes meaningful.

Survey architecture: capturing the complete journey

Rather than ask a single satisfaction question, PaxInsight captures feedback across 60+ attributes spanning eight journey stages. But asking all questions to all passengers would create fatigue and dropout. Instead, branching logic personalises: economy passengers see different questions than business. Short-haul omits lounge. Long-haul adds sleep quality. The result is a survey tailored to each passenger, with completion rates exceeding 94%.

One client discovered that business-class satisfaction was driven entirely by service consistency (crew attentiveness, meal timing) rather than seat recline or champagne selection. Another found that economy passengers on short-haul flights prioritised boarding speed over in-flight entertainment. These insights would be invisible in a generic "are you happy?" survey. They required deliberately designed questions targeting specific journey moments.

⚠ Demonstration Scope: Miniature vs. Enterprise

The survey, dashboards, and analytics above represent a simplified demonstration of PaxInsight. The actual platform delivered to clients was substantially more elaborate. Key differences:

Survey Scope: This demo shows 12 questions spanning 2 journey stages. The actual PaxInsight surveys captured 60+ distinct attributes across 8 journey stages (booking, check-in, lounge, boarding, comfort, service, baggage, arrival) with extensive branching logic and personalisation. Long-haul business passengers might see 30+ questions; short-haul economy passengers 15–20 questions; connecting passengers received additional segments. This demo has been condensed for web usability while illustrating the methodology.

Dashboard Granularity: The demo dashboards show airline-level and route-level breakdowns. Actual client dashboards drill down to: airline × route × cabin × day-of-week × departure time × flight duration × aircraft type × flight number. One client dashboard contained 87 separate visualisations, each explorable by 6+ dimensions.

Customisation: Each airline's platform was fully customized. Low-cost carriers received different questions (price perception, baggage handling). Full-service carriers received premium-focused metrics. Regional carriers received turnaround-speed questions. Branching logic, report templates, and dashboard layouts were tailored to each airline's strategic priorities.

Analysis Depth: The demo shows monthly trends. Actual reporting included week-over-week analysis, cohort comparisons (frequent flyers vs. occasional), route-pair analysis, competitor win/loss analysis, and predictive NPS modelling. Client reports regularly ran 18+ pages with 40+ visualisations.

Data Scale: This demo uses ~4,000 monthly responses. Large airlines generated 12,000–15,000+ monthly responses, enabling granularity down to individual aircraft and crew scheduling patterns. This demo represents 25–30% of actual operational scale.

The purpose of this demo is to illustrate the approach and methodology. The actual PaxInsight platform was designed for enterprise-scale customer intelligence, with security, compliance, and customisation features beyond this simplified example.

Impact: Data into Strategy

Over 12 months, I delivered monthly intelligence products to three major airlines. Each package included an 8–12 page executive report (new insights, trend analysis, benchmarking), real-time Power BI dashboards (drill-down capability, export-to-PDF), and ad-hoc analysis (client questions answered within 48 hours). The work translated data into measurable business outcomes: operational improvements validated by data, product investments justified by benchmarking, and strategic route optimisations informed by passenger satisfaction analysis.
